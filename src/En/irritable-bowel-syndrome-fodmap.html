Understanding IBS

What is Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)?

Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) is a chronic functional gastrointestinal disorder. This means that while there is no visible disease or damage in the gut, its function is impaired. IBS is characterized by recurring abdominal pain, bloating, and changes in bowel habits (diarrhea, constipation, or a mix of both).

It is a common condition affecting 7-15% of the global population, more prevalent in women. While not life-threatening, IBS can significantly impact one's quality of life.

Understanding IBS: Symptoms & Causes

A holistic approach to understanding IBS involves recognizing both the symptoms you experience and the underlying factors that contribute to this condition.

Common Symptoms
Abdominal Pain

Pain or cramping, often related to bowel movements.

Stool Changes

Changes in frequency and consistency of bowel movements.

Bloating

Sensation of abdominal fullness and distension.

Excess Gas

Increased flatulence and gas production.

Fatigue

Chronic fatigue and persistent tiredness affecting daily activities.

Underlying Causes
Altered Motility

Changes in gastrointestinal movement patterns.

Pain Sensitivity

Increased pain perception in the gut (visceral hypersensitivity).

Brain-Gut Axis

Disturbances in the brain-gut communication system.

Gut Microbiota

Changes in the balance of gut bacteria and microorganisms.

Low-Grade Inflammation

Subtle inflammation in the intestinal lining.

A formal diagnosis from a medical doctor is essential

to rule out other conditions (like Celiac disease or IBD) before starting a dietary approach. Once IBS is diagnosed, exploring nutritional factors like FODMAPs is a primary pathway to regaining control and improving your quality of life.

Understanding FODMAPs

Understanding FODMAPs: The Hidden Triggers

Think of FODMAPs as certain types of carbs that your gut has trouble digesting. When you have IBS, these carbs can cause problems by pulling extra water into your intestines and getting quickly broken down by bacteria, which creates gas and discomfort.

F

Fermentable

These are carbs that your gut bacteria love to feast on, creating gas as they break them down. Think of it like a party in your intestines! All the groups below are fermentable, but some people with IBS are more sensitive to this process than others.

O
Oligosaccharides

These are found in wheat, onions, garlic, and legumes. They're one of the most common triggers for IBS symptoms.

Common High-FODMAP Foods
Grains & Breads
Wheat Rye Barley
Vegetables

Legumes
Beans Lentils Chickpeas
D
Disaccharides (Lactose)

This is the sugar found in milk and dairy products. Many people with IBS have trouble digesting it because they don't have enough of the enzyme that breaks it down.

Common High-Lactose Foods
Dairy Products
Cow's Milk Goat's Milk Yogurt Soft Cheeses
Processed Foods
Ice Cream Cream Butter Milk Chocolate
M
Monosaccharides (Excess Fructose)

This is the natural sugar found in fruits and sweeteners. It's only a problem when there's more fructose than glucose in a food, which can overwhelm your gut's ability to absorb it.

Common High-Fructose Foods
High-Fructose Fruits
Apples Pears Mango Watermelon
Sweeteners & Syrups
Honey HFCS Agave Syrup Fruit Juice
P
Polyols (Sugar Alcohols)

These are sugar alcohols that your body doesn't absorb well. They can pull water into your intestines and get fermented by bacteria, causing bloating and gas.

Common High-Polyol Foods
Stone Fruits

Vegetables
Mushrooms
Artificial Sweeteners

Don't worry about the "A" in FODMAP — it just means "and"! It's just there to connect all these different types of carbs together. It's not a separate group you need to remember.

How Do FODMAPs Cause Symptoms?

FODMAPs cause problems in two main ways that can trigger your IBS symptoms:

1. Osmotic Effect

FODMAPs act like a sponge, pulling extra water into your intestines. This can make your stools looser, give you that urgent feeling to go to the bathroom, and make your belly feel bloated and uncomfortable.

2. Fermentation

Since your body doesn't fully absorb FODMAPs, they end up in your large intestine where your gut bacteria go to town on them! This creates gas, bloating, and that uncomfortable feeling in your belly.

Here's the good news!

Understanding how FODMAPs work means you can take control! By working with an expert to follow a Low FODMAP diet, you can significantly reduce these triggers and get real relief from your symptoms.

3-Phase Journey

The Low FODMAP Diet: A 3-Phase Journey to Relief

The Low FODMAP diet is a proven approach developed by Monash University to help you figure out which foods trigger your symptoms. Think of it as a detective game for your gut! It's not meant to be a forever diet, but rather a tool to help you find long-term relief.

1

Phase 1: Elimination

For 4 weeks, we'll help you temporarily avoid high-FODMAP foods to give your gut a break and see how much better you feel. This helps us establish a baseline of comfort.

2

Phase 2: Reintroduction

Once you're feeling better, we'll carefully test each FODMAP group one by one to figure out exactly which foods and how much of them trigger your symptoms. This is where we get really personal with your diet!

3

Phase 3: Personalization

Based on what we discover, we'll create a long-term diet plan that's as varied and enjoyable as possible. You'll only need to avoid the specific foods that actually bother you, not everything!
